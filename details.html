Details & Methodology

How the model works, why it’s built this way, and how well it has performed historically.

Why use futures prices?

Oil futures markets aggregate the expectations of thousands of traders — refiners locking in supply costs, airlines hedging fuel bills, financial investors taking directional bets. The consensus price embedded in a futures contract is therefore the market’s best collective guess about where prices will be at a given date in the future.

This doesn’t mean futures are always right. No model predicts geopolitical shocks, pandemics, or sudden OPEC policy reversals. But futures represent a continuously updated, publicly visible, financially-backed baseline — considerably more rigorous than any single analyst’s projection.

Why Brent, not WTI?

There are two main global crude benchmarks: WTI (West Texas Intermediate, produced in the US Midcontinent) and Brent (produced in the North Sea, used as the international reference price). Before the US shale boom around 2011, they tracked closely. After shale flooded the Midcontinent faster than export infrastructure could handle it, WTI developed a persistent discount to Brent.

US retail gasoline prices are set with reference to the global market — where Brent dominates — so Brent has a materially stronger statistical relationship to US pump prices since 2011. This model uses Brent as its primary benchmark. When displaying WTI, a +$4/bbl adjustment is added to approximate the Brent-equivalent price before converting to pump estimates.

The lag model: crude to pump prices

Crude oil doesn’t appear at the pump the next day. It takes time to ship to refineries, refine into gasoline, blend with ethanol, transport through pipelines to distribution terminals, and finally reach retail stations. Econometric analysis of weekly EIA data finds that crude oil price changes take roughly 6–9 weeks to fully pass through to retail gasoline prices.

The model fits a distributed lag specification: pumpₜ = α + ∑(βₐ × crudeₜ₋ₐ / 42), summing over 8 weekly lags. The total pass-through is typically around 0.75 — meaning a sustained $1/bbl change in crude eventually results in roughly $0.018/gallon at the pump ($1 ÷ 42 × 0.75). A live crack spread correction (the current refining margin vs. its historical average) is applied on top.

No autoregressive term is included — the model must be applicable to future dates where pump prices are not yet known.

How wide are the uncertainty bands?

The bands use 30% annualised volatility on both the upside and downside — calibrated against 20 years of historical futures prediction errors. Backtesting shows that roughly 68% of realized prices fell within the inner band and ~95% within the outer band, broadly matching the target coverage rates.

Past roughly 2.5 years the bands stop widening: long-run mean reversion kicks in, as prices tend to gravitate back toward production costs (~$50–70/bbl for most global producers at current technology).

Limitations

No model reliably forecasts geopolitical shocks (a sudden supply embargo, a war that closes a shipping lane, an unexpected OPEC+ decision) or major demand shocks (a pandemic, a rapid shift in EV adoption). The uncertainty bands are designed to convey honest humility about a deeply complex system — not false precision.

The bands are derived from 20 years of historical volatility and will be systematically too narrow in genuine tail events. The pump price lag model is re-estimated from two years of data and may drift when refining economics or retail market structure changes significantly.

Did the prediction bands hold up historically?

For every historical week since 2004, the model computed uncertainty bands around the futures price, then checked whether the realized spot price fell inside them. Well-calibrated bands should hit ~68% and ~95% coverage respectively.
