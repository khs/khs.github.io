Details & Methodology

How the model works, why it’s built this way, and how well it has performed historically.

Why use futures prices?

Oil futures markets aggregate the price expectations of thousands of traders — refiners locking in supply costs, airlines hedging fuel bills, financial investors taking directional bets. The price embedded in a futures contract is therefore the market’s revealed, financially-backed consensus for where prices will be on a given date.

Empirical research offers a nuanced picture: Alquist and Kilian[2] find that crude oil futures do not systematically outperform a simple no-change (random-walk) forecast, particularly at horizons beyond a few months. Hamilton[7] similarly documents how much of the crude price series is consistent with an unpredictable random walk driven by shocks. This model therefore treats futures prices as a market consensus — the best publicly available, continuously updated baseline — rather than as a reliable predictor. The uncertainty bands exist precisely because the future is genuinely uncertain.

Why Brent, not WTI?

There are two main global crude benchmarks: WTI (West Texas Intermediate, US Midcontinent) and Brent (North Sea, international reference). Before the US shale boom they tracked closely. After 2011, a surge in unconventional production from the Williston Basin and Permian Basin flooded Midcontinent pipeline capacity, creating a large and persistent WTI discount to Brent. Borenstein and Kellogg[6] document this infrastructure bottleneck and its consequences for US regional crude prices; Kilian[8] traces the spread’s evolution through the shale revolution.

Because US retail gasoline is priced off the global market (where Brent dominates), Brent has a materially stronger statistical relationship to US pump prices post-2011. This model uses Brent as its primary benchmark. WTI prices are shown with a +$4/bbl adjustment to approximate the Brent-equivalent before converting to pump estimates.

The lag model: crude to pump prices

Crude oil does not appear at the pump immediately. It must be shipped to refineries, cracked into gasoline, blended with ethanol, moved through pipelines, and finally reach retail stations. The pass-through of crude price changes to retail gasoline prices therefore plays out over several weeks. Borenstein, Cameron, and Gilbert[5] provide the canonical empirical study of this relationship, documenting both the lag structure and significant asymmetries (discussed below).

The model fits a finite distributed lag (FDL) specification over weekly data:[5]

pumpt = α + β0 (crudet/42) + β1 (crudet−1/42) + … + β8 (crudet−8/42) + εt

Dividing crude by 42 converts $/barrel to $/gallon (one barrel = 42 US gallons). The constant α captures average non-crude costs: taxes, distribution, retail margin, and the long-run average refining margin. The coefficients βk measure the share of a crude price change that passes through to pump prices after k weeks; their sum Σβk is the total pass-through, typically around 0.70–0.80 in EIA data.

Weekly crude prices are highly autocorrelated, making adjacent lag values nearly collinear. When the maximum pairwise correlation among lags exceeds 0.95, the model switches to a Polynomial Distributed Lag (PDL) estimator due to Almon,[1] which constrains the βk to lie on a low-degree polynomial in k. This stabilises the individual coefficient estimates while preserving an unbiased estimate of total pass-through.

Rockets-and-feathers test. Bacon[3] coined the term “rockets and feathers” to describe the empirical regularity that retail gasoline prices rise quickly when crude rises (“rockets”) but fall slowly when crude falls (“feathers”). Borenstein, Cameron, and Gilbert[5] confirm this asymmetry for the US market. The model tests for asymmetry by adding a single indicator term:

Dt × Δcrudet, where Dt = 1 if crude rose that week, 0 otherwise

A significantly positive coefficient on this term would indicate faster upward pass-through. The test is informational: the symmetric specification is retained regardless, because the short two-year estimation window contains too few large price swings to estimate asymmetric dynamics reliably. The symmetric model is appropriate for the forward-looking forecast use case.

No autoregressive (AR) term is included. The model must produce forecasts for future dates where pump prices are not yet observed, so lagged pump prices cannot appear as regressors.

The uncertainty bands

If the logarithm of future oil prices follows a random walk with drift — the core assumption of Black’s[4] commodity futures pricing model — then over a horizon of T years the log-price is normally distributed with standard deviation σ√T. The ±1σ interval in price space is therefore:

upper1σ = F × exp(+σ × √T)
lower1σ = F × exp(−σ × √T)

where F is today’s futures price and σ is the annualised volatility (here calibrated to 0.33, or 33%). The ±2σ bands substitute 2σ for σ. The inner band targets ~68% coverage and the outer band ~95% — the conventional one- and two-standard-deviation probability intervals for a normal distribution.

Mean-reversion cap. For very long horizons the σ√T formula implies indefinitely widening uncertainty, which contradicts the empirical behaviour of commodity prices. Schwartz[10] shows that commodity prices exhibit mean reversion: deviations from a long-run equilibrium level decay at a rate governed by a speed-of-adjustment parameter κ, so the long-run variance is proportional to σ²/(2κ) rather than growing without bound. Pindyck[9] documents that real energy prices do indeed revert toward a slowly-drifting long-run trend tied to production costs. To capture this parsimoniously, the effective horizon is capped at 2.5 years:

Teff = min(T, 2.5)
band = F × exp(±σ × √Teff)

Past 2.5 years the bands plateau rather than continuing to widen. The 33% volatility figure is calibrated empirically: backtesting against 20 years of EIA futures and spot data finds that this value produces ~68% coverage in the inner band and ~95% in the outer band.

Limitations

No model reliably forecasts geopolitical shocks (a supply embargo, a war that closes a shipping lane, an unexpected OPEC+ decision) or major demand shocks (a pandemic, a rapid shift in EV adoption). The uncertainty bands are designed to convey honest humility about a deeply complex system — not false precision.

The bands are derived from historical volatility and will be systematically too narrow in genuine tail events. The pump price lag model is re-estimated from two years of weekly data and may drift when refining economics, retail market structure, or the US crude export regime changes significantly.

Did the prediction bands hold up historically?

For every historical week since 2004, the model computed uncertainty bands around the futures-implied price, then checked whether the realized price fell inside them. Well-calibrated bands should hit ~68% and ~95% coverage respectively.
