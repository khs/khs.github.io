Trump's War

The U.S. strike on Iran didn't just make the news. It made you pay $1.47 more per gallon — and the market isn't expecting a refund.

Through most of 2025, Americans caught a break at the pump. Oil supply was ample, demand from China had softened, and crude had spent the year drifting downward. By December, WTI crude touched $58 a barrel — its lowest in years — and the national average for a gallon of regular gasoline fell below $3. For a country that runs on driving, that was real money back in real pockets.

Then came the war. The Trump administration's strikes on Iran sent crude markets into shock. Oil surged by more than $60 a barrel in weeks. Brent crude, which drives U.S. pump prices, crossed $118. The summer driving season — which adds roughly 12 cents a gallon in blending costs — arrived on top of an already elevated base.

At the national average, Americans are paying about $735 more per year to fill their tanks than they were last December.

Today the national average is around $4.34 a gallon. For the average American household — which burns through roughly 500 gallons a year — that's about $735 more annually compared to December. Households in the West and California are paying considerably more.

Grey line: historical pump price (EIA monthly WTI, converted to estimated retail). Blue line: futures-market-implied pump price (Brent forward curve). Dark band: 68% probability range  |  Light band: 95% range.

The chart above shows where prices have been, where they are today, and where the futures market — where professional traders put real money behind their views — expects them to go. The market is not expecting a quick return to pre-war levels. Brent crude contracts through 2027 remain priced above $100 a barrel, which implies pump prices well above $3.75 nationally for the foreseeable future.

The shaded bands reflect honest uncertainty: oil markets are volatile and genuinely hard to predict. A deep global recession, a negotiated settlement, or a sudden surge in non-OPEC supply could push prices lower. But even the optimistic tail of the 95% band — the lower edge of the light shading — shows only modest relief from current levels over the next year. The market is not pricing in a peace dividend.

What the market is pricing in is the new normal: a Middle East where the risk premium on oil is structurally higher, refinery margins are elevated, and the era of $2.50 gas belongs to a world that existed before the first missile flew.
