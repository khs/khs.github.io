Trump's War

The U.S. strike on Iran didn't just make the news. It made you pay $1.47 more per gallon. The market isn't expecting a refund.

In early 2026, the Trump administration launched airstrikes on Iranian nuclear facilities. Iran responded within days: mines went into the Strait of Hormuz, proxy militias attacked Gulf tanker traffic, and insurance rates on Middle East shipping spiked. The Strait carries roughly 20 percent of the world's oil. Brent crude crossed $118 a barrel. You're paying the difference every time you fill up.

How I got the numbers

The pump price forecasts come from Brent crude futures traded on the NYMEX. These are contracts where traders actually put money on what oil will cost in future months. I convert the futures price to a pump price using a statistical model trained on ten years of EIA data, which accounts for the typical lag between crude prices and what shows up at the station, plus seasonal blending costs.

Questions

If the U.S. exports oil, why do Americans pay more?

Because crude oil is a global commodity with a global price. When an American refiner buys crude to turn into gasoline, it pays the world market price, whether that crude came from Texas, Saudi Arabia, or Norway. U.S. producers don't offer a domestic discount; they sell to whoever pays the most, which is how commodity markets work.

When the Strait of Hormuz is disrupted, roughly 20 percent of global supply is threatened. That tightens the global market, which raises the global price, which raises what your refiner pays, which raises what you pay at the pump. Being an oil exporter means the U.S. economy benefits when prices are high (producers make more money), but it does not insulate American consumers from high prices.

Why can't we just drill more to bring prices down?

Drilling takes time, capital, and confidence that prices will stay high long enough to justify the investment. A new well takes 6 to 18 months to complete after a company commits to it. Companies also know that a peace deal or a recession could collapse prices before that well pays off, so they wait for a clearer signal.

There's also a supply-demand arithmetic problem. U.S. oil production is already near record highs. Even a significant increase in output over a year or two would represent a few percent of global supply, not enough to offset a 20 percent disruption in Hormuz transit. OPEC spare capacity could fill the gap faster, but OPEC members are also benefiting from high prices and have little incentive to rescue American consumers.

What about releasing oil from the Strategic Petroleum Reserve?

The SPR holds roughly 700 million barrels, about 35 days of total U.S. petroleum consumption. The Biden administration released about 180 million barrels in 2022 during the Ukraine price spike and got roughly 15 to 25 cents of per-gallon relief that lasted a few months before prices recovered. It's a pressure valve for short-term supply shocks, not a mechanism for holding prices down against a sustained geopolitical premium.

The SPR also cannot replace the Strait of Hormuz. The Strait handles about 21 million barrels a day. The entire SPR is 700 million barrels. You can do the math.

Could a ceasefire or peace deal bring prices back down quickly?

Yes, and fast. Oil markets are forward-looking. If the Strait reopened and tanker insurance normalized, Brent crude could fall $30 to $50 a barrel within days, and pump prices would follow within a few weeks given the lag in the refining and distribution chain. The uncertainty bands on the chart reflect this: the lower edge of the 95 percent band represents a scenario where conditions improve substantially.

What the market is saying, though, is that the base case is elevated prices through at least 2026. Traders price in the probability of relief; right now they're not assigning much to it.

Why are prices so much higher in California than the Gulf Coast?

Two things: taxes and refinery geography. California requires a special reformulated gasoline blend with tighter emissions specifications than federal standards. Only a handful of refineries on the West Coast can produce it. When one of those refineries goes offline for maintenance or an accident, there's no easy way to import replacement supply from the Gulf Coast or overseas. Prices spike hard and stay high.

California also has the highest state gas tax in the country, around 68 cents per gallon, plus additional low-carbon fuel surcharges. The Gulf Coast has lower taxes and sits next door to the largest refining complex in the country, which keeps prices structurally lower.

Why does April get more expensive every year?

Every spring, refineries switch from winter-blend to summer-blend gasoline. Summer blend burns more cleanly in hot weather, which matters for air quality regulations, but it's more expensive to produce: it requires additional refining steps and different additives. The switchover typically adds 10 to 20 cents a gallon and happens right around April, which is why you see prices climb even when crude oil hasn't moved.

On top of that, spring marks the start of driving season. Demand picks up as the weather improves and people start taking longer trips, putting upward pressure on prices just as refineries are mid-transition and running lean on inventory. The combination reliably produces a spring price spike most years, war or no war. The forecasts on this page account for that seasonal pattern so the war's cost is measured on top of what you'd have paid anyway.

How accurate are these forecasts?

Futures markets are the best available public forecast of future commodity prices, but they're not precise. In 20 years of backtests on WTI crude, 1-month futures missed the realized price by an average of $8 to $10 a barrel, and realized prices fell outside the 68 percent band roughly 32 percent of the time (close to the theoretical 32 percent, which is reassuring). At 4-month horizons the error roughly doubles.

The pump price forecast adds an additional source of error on top of the crude error: the relationship between crude prices and retail gasoline prices is stable over time but not perfectly predictable month to month. Crack spreads, refinery outages, and regional supply bottlenecks all introduce noise. The bands shown here are calibrated to the historical distribution of those errors, so they represent honest uncertainty, not false precision.
